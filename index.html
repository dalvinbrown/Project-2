As NYC aims to end HIV by 2020, black gay men in the Bronx face layered inequalities that put them at higher risk.
By Dalvin Brown
Published Sept. 11, 2019 8:00 PM

As a child, Dorian Banks was captivated by the show “Noah’s Arc,” a 2005 cable dramedy that followed a cast of black, gay men living in Los Angeles. “I wanted to be just like them,” Banks, now 25, said. .

He moved to New York from Detroit in his late teens, hoping to become a dancer. He settled in the Fordham section of the Bronx where he wound up lacking housing security, dabbling in sex work and living out a storyline from “Noah’s Arc” that he hadn’t planned for.

O“I was diagnosed with HIV. I was ‘Junito,’” a character on the show, Banks said. Black men who have sex with men are far more affected by HIV than any other group in the United States. And CDC data suggests 50% of them will contract the disease in their lifetime.

In the Bronx, where HIV transmission rates are highest in the city, the community faces layers of inequalities that increase their likelihood of HIV infection. Still, Banks is represented among a more promising statistic: He caught the disease early.

Between Banks’ diagnosis in 2014, and the latest data available in 2017, timely initiation of care among HIV-positive people in the Bronx rose from 74% to 84%, according to the health department.

Early diagnosis is one of the key drivers of successful treatment and it reduces the likelihood of further transmission, according to Oni Blackstock, assistant commissioner of HIV/AIDS prevention and control for the health department. Fewer than 10-years ago, testing a sample of blood for HIV took days. Today, rapid tests can complete the task in 20 minutes, Blackstock said.

Testing facilities have also become easier to come during that time.

Since 2007, the number of city-sponsored testing sites in the Bronx has doubled to more than 160, according to the health department. There are at least five HIV-testing centers in the Fordham area, including the Montefiore outpost that treats Banks.

Housing stability also plays a vital role in both treating and preventing HIV among his peers. "Young, black and brown queer people are well represented, unfortunately, among the homeless population in the city,” Blackstock said. She spent 3-years conducting HIV research at Montefiore in the South Bronx and Fordham area. Gay people younger than 26 often lack family support and have difficulty accessing healthcare coverage without a parent. They also face homophobia and transphobia, Blackstock said.

“These factors put young people in positions where they may be exchanging sex for money or for food, which puts them at risk for acquiring HIV and other (sexually transmitted diseases),” Blackstock said. Injection drug use is also a driver of HIV transmission among young, black men in the city's northernmost borough. Van Asher, who is the head of a syringe access program at St. Ann's Corner of Harm Reduction in the South Bronx, explained how government-sponsored programs curb HIV-infection rates among young adults through outreach. “Our outreach team will text them to remind them to come to their appointments, and some programs have late evening or weekend operating hours so people can come in when they’re out of school,” Asher said.
